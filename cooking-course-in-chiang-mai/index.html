01 Oct 2014 • on Thailand • by Kaitlyn Richards

Cooking Course in Chiang Mai

One of the things that Alan and I had on our list of things we wanted to do while in Thailand was take a cooking course. We both love Thai food and thought it would be fun to learn how to make it, so when we get home we can make some yummy food.

We researched a couple different cooking schools in Chiang Mai (there are lots!) and settled on Baan Thai Cookery School, which is located not far from where we were staying. Each person taking the course gets to choose an individual menu based on what they want to learn to cook. We cooked six items in total:

  • a curry paste (red, green, or paenang), which was then used in
  • a curry (green curry, paenang curry, or Chiang Mai noodle with chicken),
  • a stir fry dish (fried noodle Thai style, friend cashew nut with chicken, or stir fried prawn with curry powder),
  • a soup (hot and sour prawn soup, chicken in coconut milk, or seafood in coconut milk),
  • an appetizer (papaya salad, spring rolls, or fried fish cakes),
  • a dessert (deep fried banana, mango with sticky rice, or water chestnuts in coconut milk).

They picked us up in the morning, along with another woman (small class!) and our first stop was the market to pick up the ingredients we would be using. The three girls from the school were very helpful explaining all the different foods, spices, veggies, etc., as well as what they were used for and the differences between certain similar items. We had a bit of time to wander around the market by ourselves as well, and then ask questions about what items were or what they were used for.

After we picked up our ingredients for the day, we headed off to the cooking school. We started off by tasting a variety of Thai snacks and fruit. I hadn’t had any of it before, so I thought it was cool that they did that for us. The mangosteen was pretty cool.

We started off with the stir fry dish. Alan and I both chose the fried noodle Thai style (aka Pad Thai). It was pretty simple to make (really quick!) and we got to eat it right away after it was done.

The second dish we made was the appetizer. For this, I chose to make the spring rolls while Alan made the papaya salad. I thought the spring rolls might be tricky, but they weren’t hard at all. I made five (one of which was massive)! I definitely couldn’t eat all that, so I left with a couple in a doggy bag for dinner.

The soup was the next dish we made; I chose the hot and sour prawn soup, and Alan chose the chicken in coconut milk. I wasn’t a huge fan of the soup; it was just okay. At this point everyone was feeling pretty full as well! We had a small break after eating the soup to rest/go for a walk, before coming back to make the final dishes.

After the break, we learned the differences between regular rice (such as jasmine) and sticky rice (we had all chosen the mango sticky rice for the dessert option). The jasmine rice is translucent looking, turning white when it’s cooked. Sticky rice is the opposite - it’s white to begin with, and turns translucent when cooked. You have to soak the rice for at least four hours (preferably overnight), so you need to know in advance that you’re going to be making this dish! That part was already done, so we just added the coconut milk and sugar. The Thai like their food super sweet; we only put in a fraction of the sugar that would normally go in, and it was pretty sweet already! The rice has to soak in the coconut mixture for around 20-30 minutes, so we left that to soak while we moved on to the final dish.

We also got to try making coconut milk from scratch. This isn't done normally here, but it was interesting to learn. Basically you take old coconut and squish it up to get the cream out, then mix with water to make the milk. We used it in our curries next!

Alan and I both chose green curry for our curry dish. We made the curry paste from scratch using garlic, shallot, fresh chillies, lemongrass, coriander root, turmeric root, and galangai (Thai ginger). It was a lot of pounding the ingredients together with the mortar and pestle to get the paste.

After that, we cooked up the curry. I was a bit worried that it would be too spicy for me (with the nine chillies that we put in), but I just ended up adding less of the paste to my curry and it turned out perfectly! It was probably my favourite of the meals we made.

The instructors brought out the mango sticky rice as we were finishing up with the curry. It was really yummy! The perfect amount of sweetness. By this point we were all stuffed, but it was too good not to eat!

Overall it was a really fun day; we learned a lot about Thai cooking and what the ingredients are used for. We also were sent home with a cookbook so that we can try making the dishes (the ones we made and even the ones we didn’t) at home.
