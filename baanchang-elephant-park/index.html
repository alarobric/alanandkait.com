04 Oct 2014 • on Thailand • by Alan Richards

Baanchang Elephant Park

We hummed and hawed a lot about this. Chiang Mai is well known for the elephant parks here, and it's something we wanted to do on our trip. The cost of one of these is pretty high though, at least compared to our daily budget here ($80 each when our overall budget is $50/day here). The animal treatment can be pretty questionable sometimes too, so we did a lot of research and read lots of reviews. We ended up choosing Baanchang Elephant Park as they seemed to treat the elephants as best they could.

Our day started by being picked up from our guesthouse and driven an hour or so north of the city. We drove onto the property and saw about 40 or so elephants, but before we could do much, we had to change into our mahout outfits. Mahout is the Hindi term for someone who rides or trains elephants, and we were learning to be mahouts for the day.

We then learnt a little about elephant bodies, and got to feed them a mid-morning snack of bananas and sugarcane.

You'll notice each elephant has its own mahout. They spend all day every day together for years at a time. Being a mahout is not a job for someone who wants to see their family, as they work 12 hours a day and must live next door to the elephants to care for them round the clock if necessary.

We also got some elephant kisses from a friendly elephant!

After this we learnt some basic commands to control the elephants:

  • Na long - Sit down (used to climb on or off the elephant)
  • Pai - Go/walk
  • Quai - Turn (confusingly this is either direction)
  • How - Stop
  • Dee - Good job, good girl, etc. along with a pat on the trunk

We took it in turns to climb up on an elephant and walk a very short loop to get used to riding on the elephant.

We then broke for lunch - some Pad Thai - and a rest in a hammock.

After lunch we mounted up and went for a longer hike on the elephants. This time we were two to an elephant. Kaitlyn and I switched partway so we each got to ride at the front and back. It was much less scary and easier to balance at the back, but an elephants back is much wider than its neck, and the prickly hairs hurt our legs a bit, so overall I preferred sitting at the front. We walked a large loop around the land they own here, with the mahouts walking nearby and helping control the elephants if we needed it.

Our final task was to wash and bathe the elephants. We jumped in the pond with them with buckets and scrubbing brushes and helped to clean them. Of course the mahouts also got them to spray us with water as well! We were pretty soaked by the end of it.

All in all we thought it was a great experience. The elephants are indeed chained in place by one foot much of the time, and the mahouts carry the pointy stick used to control them, but this is hardly necessary most of the time. The elephants are mostly all rescues from forestry camps, and it's clear their lives are better here. They are well cared for, and the mahouts treat them well, getting them exercise, baths, daily medical checks, etc.
