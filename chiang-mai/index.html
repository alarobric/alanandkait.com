03 Oct 2014 • on Thailand • by Alan Richards

Chiang Mai

We spent a week in Chiang Mai and, despite being Thailand's second biggest city after Bangkok, we found it a pleasant, quiet and green city. After looking at the prices online, we decided it was worth the little bit of extra money to fly directly here from Krabi, instead of spending almost two full days on the trains (one from Krabi to Bangkok and then another from Bangkok to Chiang Mai).

The old city is where most visitors stay; it's a quiet area of small lanes, guesthouses, and many, many wats. Surrounding the outside is a moat with two ring-roads. The inside goes counter-clockwise and the outside vice versa with a few cross-over points on each side of the city.

Not much of the old walls survive - just a few small sections at the main gates and each corner.

Outside the old city Chiang Mai is much busier. There are markets all over selling food, clothing, and everything else. The traffic can get pretty bad though, and we spent a while stuck in traffic the few times we ventured out of the city.

The exception to this is the mountain directy west of the city. Known as Doi Suthep, and mostly covered by a national park, it's only a few kilometers out of town. We rented a scooter one day and drove the 17km winding well-paved road most of the way to the top.

There are essentially three points of interest on the mountain:

  • Wat Phra That Doi Suthep
  • Bhubing Palace
  • Hill-tribe village

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep

The first thing you find on the drive up Doi Suthep is this wat. Built in 1383 the site was allegedly picked by a sacred white elephant. Left to roam, it eventually climbed to the site of the temple to be, trumpeted three times, turned around three times, knelt down and died. This was taken as an auspicious sign, and the first chedi of the temple was built.

The temple itself is up a naga-lined 300-step staircase. The views from the top over Chiang Mai are great though!

Bhubing Palace

This is a royal palace located up on the hill above Chiang Mai, and built to accomodate the royal family and visiting dignitaries while they were in the North. The palace is open to the public whenever the royal family is not visiting. Although the buildings aren't particularly interesting, the gardens are very nice, especially the famous rose garden. The dress code is very strict compared to most Thai Wats, and although we had full-length pants, Kaitlyn's shawl was not enough and she had to rent a shirt.

Hill-tribe village

Farther up the road again is a Hmong hill-tribe village. This one is a bit commercial due to its proximity to Chiang Mai, but we had a short wander through the village looking at the stalls.

The old city has about a million wats inside, and we visited some of the major ones.

  • Wat Chiang Man - the oldest wat in the city dating from the 13th century. It contains two important Buddha images, although to be honest it wasn't very clear to us which those were.

  • Wat Phra Sing - dates from 1345 and has some nice grounds and Northern Thai architecture.

  • Wat Chedi Luang - my favourite; this is a huge chedi in the centre of the old town that collapsed in an earthquake a few centuries ago. In the wat nearby we saw the monks at evening prayer.

We also rented a scooter again for a long 100km circuit loop around Doi Suthep, known as the Samoeng Loop. Our bike had no problems with the windy roads and steep hills, although I was a little concerned with the brakes on the way down at times! The parts through Chiang Mai were busy, dusty and chaotic, but once on the side roads and in the mountain it was quiet and incredibly scenic. We stopped at one point to put rain jackets on before a downpour! Luckily it was shortlived and we continued into Samoeng. We had trouble finding anywhere to eat - almost everything was shut and we ended up with instant soup from 7-eleven.

The section of road leaving Samoeng to the north was by far the best, with views into lush green valleys and the sun starting to get low in the sky. We continued through some small towns and into the tourist valley of Mae Rim where many of the excusions from Chiang Mai are located. We got back into the bustle of the city around nightfall, just in time for dinner.

We visited the Sunday market in the old town. A huge never-ending market along two of the main streets. I think we spent something like 6 hours here - we did buy almost all our souvenirs for Thailand though.

While in Chiang Mai we also spent a large chunk of money on two activities that deserve their own posts: Baanchang Elephant Park and a cooking class
