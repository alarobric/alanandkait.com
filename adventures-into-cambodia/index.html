07 Oct 2014 • on Cambodia • by Kaitlyn Richards

Adventures into Cambodia...

Getting from Thailand to Cambodia was quite the adventure.

We woke up super early to catch the 5:50am train from Bangkok to Aranyaprathet, which is the town on the Thailand side of the border.

The train ride itself, although pretty long and incredibly slow (six hours to go about 250km), was actually nice. Really cheap, too - $1.65 CAD each. It had pretty scenery and didn't start getting too hot until we were getting closer to Aranyaprathet. No AC, just open windows and a fan. Lots of people only got on for a stop or two (to go to the market or bring some vegetables back home). We saw some interesting packages, but none of the quintessential chickens. Very few people stayed on with us the whole way from Bangkok to Aranyaprathet.

After you get off the train, you have to figure out how to avoid the mountain of scams that arise before you. We researched this a lot online before we left so that we'd be prepared and know what to do and not to do once we got here.

The tuk tuk scams start as soon as you get off the train. Basically they will try to get you to buy a visa through them, which involves a long drawn-out process, more money than it should, and the possibility of you having to visit all of their friends' shops/restaurant/massage parlours/etc. before you are finally taken to the border. The best thing to do is just ignore all of them and take a songthaew, or tell them you already have a visa, in which case they should take you straight to the border and then go look for a less-informed forigner. We took a songthaew from the train to the border so that we didn't have to deal with this.

Once you get to the border, you have to avoid even more people that are trying to sell you a visa. Same as the tuk tuks, just ignore them or tell them you already have one. Then you have to exit Thailand. This is pretty simple, and just involves going into a building and standing in a long line.

After you are out of Thailand, you're in the land of casinos! Avoid the guy telling you that you need to fill out a health form - you don't.

This is where you can now get your Cambodian visa. AFTER you've left Thailand - that's really the key to the whole process. The scam here though is that the border guards want a 100 baht bribe. Despite the official sign on the wall saying that it's $20 USD, there's also a paper "sign" written in bubble letters saying $20 + 100 baht. We managed to talk our way out of it and only had to pay the $20 each, but the guards looked quite disappointed. We were a little scared to take a picture of it, but this blog we read beforehand drew a replica picture of it afterwards and it looks pretty much the same:

You need a passport photo each for the visa, but if you don't have one they just take another 100 baht bribe. We just took a photocopy of our passport we had, and cut out our photos!

Finally you get the visa, and you walk past all the casinos to the Cambodian entry. You have to fill out another form and then stand in a another super long line, where you have to give your fingerprints before being granted entry.

Once you're finally in Cambodia, you have a few options on how to get from Poipet (the Cambodian border town) to Siem Reap (where we were headed) - or to Phnom Penh if you're going there instead. You can take the "free shuttle" that everyone is trying to get you on, which takes you to the "tourist bus station", and then get on a bus from there. You can also arrange your own transport by taking a shared taxi (they cram up to 7 people into the one car, and wait until it fills up before leaving), paying for a whole taxi, or walking a bit until you get to other buses. The area around the border is controlled by the cartel, so only their modes of transport are allowed within a certain distance.

We decided to go for the "free shuttle", which is a bit scammy and is also run by the cartel, but reading about it online it seemed safe enough and likely just as cheap. Once you're at the bus station, you can board a bus to Siem Reap for $9. We had to wait about an hour before there was enough people to leave.

The ride to Siem Reap is supposed to take two hours, but an hour and a half in we had to stop for a "half hour toilet and dinner break", which turned into over an hour. Only a few people actually ordered any food... most of us just wanted to get to Siem Reap after our long day of travel.

In Siem Reap, the bus drops you off at a bus station out of town, and you have to negotiate with the tuk tuk drivers there in order to get a ride to your hotel.
