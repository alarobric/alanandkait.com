12 Nov 2014 • on Vietnam • by Alan Richards

Nha Trang (or 3 days in Russia)

Southern Vietnam has two well-known beach towns on the tourist trail: Mui Ne and Nha Trang. We chose to skip Mui Ne and visit Da Lat instead, but we did spend a few days in Nha Trang. Rather than struggle because we didn't speak Vietnamese, here the problem was that we didn't speak Russian!

For some reason, Nha Trang has become a vacation destination for Russians, and many if not most of the foreigners we saw here spoke what we assume was Russian or another Eastern European language. Most restaurants had Russian menus and Russian signage on the outside, usually larger than the English or Vietnamese sign, and occasionally without any English.

The town was much larger than we'd realised, a series of high rises along the beachfront. We stayed a block or so from the water in a nice quiet guesthouse off an alley. Lots of restaurants around and easy enough to walk most places.

We caught a taxi one day up to the North end of town across the river. The Po Nagar Cham Towers were built by the Cham back between the 7th and 12th centuries. Quite interesting and a nice view of town across the river. The towers are still in pretty good shape, although there are a few missing.

We then walked back into town and visited the main market. Mostly this was still a local market with food, meat, and household items, although near the end we did find the tourist section and the vendors got pushier with hawking souvenirs. The market is away from the tourist strip in the older, and still local, area of town.

We continued walking back and stopped at the Alexandre Yersin Museum. A short little museum (under half an hour even for me!), it was nevertheless very interesting. Alexandre was from France but fell in love with the area and settled here, starting a branch of the Pasteur Institute and isolating the bubonic plague.

On another day we headed south in a city bus to the Vinpearl cable car station. Vinpearl is an amusement park and resort on an island a short distance offshore. They have a ferry or cable car to get over there. We wanted to check it out, but had already decided against it due to the 500,000 dong entrance fee.

Instead we unsuccessfully searched for a nearby geocache and visited the Oceanographic Institute. A mix of pickeled animals and live fish, it was interesting and a little odd. We'd heard the animals weren't in great conditions, but most were fine from our point of view, although there were a few sad looking seals in a very small pool that you could barely see into.

Despite it being a beach town, we never really made it to the beach. On our last day, we had some time to kill before the bus so we walked over there to check it out - but unfortunately we didn't have our swimsuits with us to swim!

Alan Richards
