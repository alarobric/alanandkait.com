29 Nov 2014 • on Vietnam • by Kaitlyn Richards

Hue

The theme of our time in Hue was rain! It rained pretty much the entire time we were there.

We took a local public bus from Hoi An to Danang, where we were catching the train to Hue, because it would be cheaper than a tourist bus. The price should be 18,000 but we read online that they will pretend that it's more (up to 50,000) if you're a tourist, and will sometimes not tell you the price until you have already been traveling for a bit and are in the middle of nowhere. If you don't pay, they kick you off. I was a bit worried about this, so we asked the price before we got on: sure enough, he said 50,000 (after pausing to think about it for a minute). We decided to just pay it anyway as it was still cheaper than the other options.

The first ten minutes of the drive was so slow. I was worried we weren't going to get there until next week; the driver was literally going 5-10 km/h. He would randomly stop, take his hat off, run his hand through his hair, ponder life for a minute, and then keep going. At one point I figured his spedometer must be broken, because the needle didn't move at all between when he was stopped and when he was driving. But then I looked again a couple minutes later and it was almost at 20km/h! He sped up once he got out of town thankfully! We think he must have just been looking around the streets for more people to pick up.

It took about an hour to get to Danang, where we got on the train to Hue. The pass between Danang and Hue is supposed to be really pretty scenery; if you take a bus, it goes through the new tunnel under the pass and you miss all that. The train was a bit more expensive but I think it was worth it in the end. Those who watch Top Gear will remember it for being featured as part of the Vietnam Special.

Because of all the rain, we didn't get to see a ton in Hue; I didn't think we'd be getting this much use out of our rain jackets! Our hotel was fairly nice; the bed was incredibly uncomfortable (we had to put a blanket under us because the springs would dig into us), but the breakfast was good.

The first day in Hue, we walked over to the Imperial City. It was just on the other side of the river, not too far of a walk from our hotel. It cost 105,000 each to get in (about $5.25 CAD) and unfortunately it started pouring rain not long after we got there. We tried to wait it out for a bit but it just wasn't letting up, so we walked around in the rain anyway. The Citadel inside the Imperial City is where the emperor lived (with his 108 wives), and where all the official royal business happened. It was quite interesting to read about the different emperors and the lives they led.

When Vietnam was under French rule, the emperors didn't have all that much power anymore. Because of this, they didn't have a whole lot to do, so they built these elaborate tombs for themselves. There are three main big tombs that people go see: Tu Duc, Minh Mang, and Khai Dinh. We only had time to go to one, so we chose Tu Duc because it sounded interesting and was also the closest to town. We rented a couple of bicycles from our hotel and biked the hour or so out there.

The tomb area was huge! It had a lake, a tomb for his first wife, a building for all his concubines, and his actual tomb. There was a royal theatre for plays and quite a bit of regular business was conducted there as a sort of holiday palace.

Kaitlyn Richards
