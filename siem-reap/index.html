15 Oct 2014 • on Cambodia • by Alan Richards

Siem Reap

We spent the first week of our two weeks in Cambodia in Siem Reap. After our long journey into town, we arrived at our hotel. For CAD$13/night, we had the nicest place of our trip so far, in what we thought would be an expensive tourist trap of a town. Instead, we really enjoyed Siem Reap.

Our hotel was in a great location just one door down from one of the small night markets, and across a small pedestrian footbridge into the centre of Siem Reap. So it was only 5 minutes or less to Pub Street and tons of restaurants, yet quiet at night. We had a big room with A/C, a TV, a large nicely tiled bathroom, and cooked breakfast included. The service from the hotel staff was incredible; we’d definitely stay there again.

Obviously the main draw to Siem Reap is the Angkor temple complex, and we’ll cover that more in another post. There are hundreds of ancient temples in the area, and not all are within the Angkor Archeological Park, but there’s only so many you can see.

We had several off days between Angkor visits to relax and explore the town (and also to spend less money :P).

The only thing that made Siem Reap slightly less enjoyable was the pestering of the tuk tuk drivers. There are a ton of them, and any time you walk by any one of them you'll hear "tuk tuk? sir? lady?" - regardless of whether you're walking down the street, crossing the road, looking in a store, about to walk into a restaurant, or (my favourite) getting onto a bicycle. No we do not need a tuk tuk, sir - as you can see, we already have a form of transport with this bicycle we're sitting on. :P

Artisans

One day we visited a well-known artisans workshop. They make souvenirs and art in several different mediums, and do a good job of touring you around the workshop to see the “artisans” in progress. The work was very nice, and much better than the cheap stalls in town, but way out of our price range. It felt a bit like an assembly line where they just copy existing work. It was still worth a visit to see the different things they make and some of the details in how they are made.

Tonle Sap

We also did an organized half-day trip to Tonle Sap. This is the largest lake in South-East Asia, and is the lifeblood of Cambodia. The lake provides freshwater fish and sustenance for many. The lake itself rises and falls huge amounts with the seasons, from a depth of 2m in the dry season to 11m in the rainy season. The villages around the edges are all built on stilts, and on our boat tour we visited one of the floating villages. The entire village, including the buddhist temple, school, and shops, are all floating on barges. As the lake rises and falls, the buildings are towed into different places on the lake - up to 5km each direction a year.

These villages are quite poor however. The life expectancy at birth is 54 years, and 12% of children die before age 5. Average income per household is just US$500 per year and literacy rates are 46%. Even for Cambodia, these numbers are low. Village life still looked peaceful and idyllic from our vantage passing by.

Our tour wasn't great - we didn't spend all that long on an actual boat, and one direction it poured rain so we lowered the blinds and couldn't see anything. We spent a large part of our time on a big old riverboat they use as a base. We got lunch and two drinks, and spent time in hammocks at one end of the boat.

I enjoyed just lying there watching the villagers putter by in little canoes stacked high with vegetables to sell, fisherman casting lines, and kids playing on the barges.

We also stopped at one barge to look at souvenirs and some crocodiles in a cage. A couple local kids came by in circular tubs to offer photos with a snake and to beg. Interesting boats though!

Alan Richards
