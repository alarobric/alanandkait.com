20 Oct 2014 • on Cambodia • by Kaitlyn Richards

Phnom Penh

The bus ride from Siem Reap (see our previous posts) to Phnom Penh was along a super bumpy road, and took around seven hours or so. We took the Giant Ibis bus, which was $15 and was actually pretty nice, with free WiFi, water, and a light meal included.

Towards the end of the ride I started not feeling well, so once we checked into our hostel I took a long nap, and slept a lot of that evening/the next day. Which was a shame, because the hostel was actually pretty cool - it had a pool, and a bar/lounge area with lots of chairs and couches you could chill in. We spent a bunch of time down there writing the blog! We'd only booked two nights here because they were full after that, so we moved across town to another hotel.

The second hotel had a lot more restaurants around it; we found a good cheap one that we ended up going to a few times! There were also a lot of "happy pizza" places around; you could order a pizza with a little something extra sprinkled on top. It's apparently illegal to smoke it, but you can ingest it!

We did a few things around town: Wat Phnom is a small wat on the top of the only hill around, and is the namesake of the town.

The National Palace was right across the street from our second hotel. In the evenings, the lawn in front would fill up with Cambodian couples and families.

The waterfront and the blocks near it are where all the tourist hotels and restaurants are. There's a nice promenade though with flags from all around the world. We found the Canadian one of course!

We also managed to find a Cambodian movie theatre to watch the Maze Runner. Popcorn, pops and a deluxe couples chair for only $7! Interesting experience, even though it was a fairly early showing, when we got out around 10:30 the entire mall was shut down and we were led out this back entrance into an alley by the security guards. At home there'd be another showing starting. It's not that the city was shutting down either, other stuff would be open later.

We arranged with the same tuk tuk driver who took us over to our second hotel to take us to Tuol Sleng and the Choeung Ek Memorial (the "killing fields") the next morning. This is something I'd read about seeing in Phnom Penh, and the main thing I wanted to visit.

Tuol Sleng Genocide Museum

The first stop was the Tuol Sleng Genocide Museum. Tuol Sleng, also known as "S-21", is a former high school, used as a secret prison and interegation centre by the Khmer Rouge from 1975-1979. It was reopened in 1980 as a historical museum by the government.

Neither of us had really heard too much about the Khmer Rouge, and to read and learn about what had happened here was heartbreaking. All kinds of crazy torture methods and human experimentation was done here. There were only twelve survivors from the over 20,000 people who visited the place. Two of them were outside selling books they'd written about the experience; if that was me, those buildings would be the last place I would want to ever return to.

There were displays with the pictures of all the prisoners. The Khmer Rouge were nothing if not meticulous with their records, and they took pictures and (forced) fake statements from each prisoner when they arrived. Many prisoners were forced to confess to being CIA spies, having some Western government connection or similar backstories.

Choeung Ek Memorial

There were very few people actually killed at Tuol Sleng; the majority were brought in trucks to the "killing fields", where they built mass graves (some of which the victims had to dig themseves) and killed and buried them. Bullets were considered too valuable, so most of the time the Khmer Rouge executioners would poison them or beat them with sharpened sticks to kill them.

As part of our admission fee into the Killing Fields, we received an audio guide. It had stories from survivors, as well as information about the areas we got to walk around. The saddest part for me was seeing and hearing about the "killing tree", where they would bash the heads of children (including babies) against the tree to kill them before dumping them in the mass grave right beside it.

Many of the mass graves have not been excavated yet, and it is common to find bones, teeth, and fragments of clothing rising to the surface after heavy rains. There were displays of such fragments in one area of the museum. We didn't see any bones, but there were clothing scraps in a few of the graves.

All in all, some 20,000 people were killed at Choeung Ek during only a few short years, and there were hundreds of similar sites across the country. Somewhere around a fifth to a third of Cambodia's population was killed or died of starvation or disease during the Khmer Rouge regime, but it's not something either of us ever learnt about, or even really ever heard of.

So in the end Phnom Penh was a very different experience for us from Siem Reap. One a smaller tourism focused town with attractions based around huge architectural masterpieces from centuries ago, and the other a large modern city, rapidly growing and modernising further, but with attractions based around a horrible genocide within living memory.
